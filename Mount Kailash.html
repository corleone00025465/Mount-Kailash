Mount Kailash—Jewel of the Snows, Center of the Universe

Located in the Qiangtang Plateau of Ali, Tibet, at an altitude of approximately 6,638 meters, it is revered by Tibetan Buddhism, Hinduism, Bon, and Jainism as the "Center of the World (Mount Meru)." A single circumambulation is said to cleanse a lifetime of sins, while 108 lead to enlightenment. Embark on the path around Kailash to complete a journey into the depths of your soul.

Peak of Faith: Culture & Scenery

Myths, Symbols, and the Immortal Ice Pyramid

Sacred Significance

Mount Kailash is shaped like a magnificent "ice pyramid" with four symmetrical faces. Four major rivers—the Indus, Sutlej, Brahmaputra, and Karnali—originate from its glaciers.

  • Tibetan Buddhism: Earthly manifestation of Mount Meru, abode of the deity Chakrasamvara.
  • Hinduism: The eternal residence of Lord Shiva and his consort Parvati.
  • Bon Religion: The "Nine-Story Swastika Mountain," the spiritual heart of the ancient faith.

To preserve its purity, climbing Mount Kailash is strictly forbidden, making it the great "unconquered" sacred peak that guards an immortal spiritual mystery.

Sacred Mountain & Lakes

South of the sacred mountain, Lake Manasarovar and Lake Rakshastal coexist, forming a cosmic symbol of "one sacred, one demonic, in opposition and unity."

Lake Manasarovar, one of the world's highest freshwater lakes, is hailed as the "Jade Pool of the Western Heaven." Devotees believe its holy water can cleanse the five poisons: greed, anger, ignorance, pride, and jealousy.

Lake Rakshastal, a barren saltwater lake, symbolizes the world's impermanence and chaos. Its shifting colors and restless waves serve as a warning of life's challenges.

Forms of Pilgrimage

Beyond standard trekking, there are more profound methods of pilgrimage:

  • Inner Kora: Only accessible after 13 outer circuits, this path ventures deep into the mountain's core.
  • Prostration: Measuring the sacred path with one's body, taking 15-20 days to complete one circuit.
  • Saga Dawa Festival: On the 15th day of the 4th Tibetan month, one kora is said to equal thirteen on normal days.

The Core Challenge: Drolma La Pass

At 5,630 meters, Drolma La Pass is the highest point of the kora and a "pass of life and death." Crossing it signifies a symbolic "death and rebirth." The thin air tests the body, while the sea of prayer flags carries the wishes of countless souls, creating an unforgettable spiritual climax to the journey.

The Soul's Trial: Stone of Rebirth

During the arduous journey over Drolma La Pass, pilgrims encounter a massive rock known as the "Stone of Rebirth." It forms a natural archway, testing every devotee who passes.

Legend holds that only the sinless and devout can pass through the narrow gap beneath it. This process is seen as a test of one's karma. Squeezing through symbolizes the cleansing of a lifetime's sins, achieving spiritual purification and a new beginning. It is a dual challenge of physical flexibility and inner faith.

Preparation — High-Altitude Survival Guide

Ensuring a Safe and Smooth Pilgrimage

Permits: Foreign travelers need several permits, including the Tibet Travel Permit. Ensure your tour operator arranges these well in advance, specifying "Ali Prefecture."

Best Time: May to October. The golden seasons are May-June & September-October for fewer crowds and great weather. July-August is the rainy season but has the most available supplies.

Costs: Major expenses include entrance fees for the Kailash area, plus optional costs for hiring yaks/porters and a guide.

Acclimatization: Spend 3-5 days in Lhasa/Shigatse upon arrival, and another 1-2 days at Darchen (the trek's starting point) before starting. Do not rush.

Essential Medicine: Portable oxygen, Diamox (Acetazolamide), Ibuprofen (for headaches), and medication for stomach issues.

Pacing: Maintain a steady, slow pace. Always go slower than you think you need to. Drink plenty of hot water, eat small, frequent meals, and avoid overeating.

Emergencies: If severe symptoms of altitude sickness appear, you must stop immediately and descend. Always trek with a companion.

Clothing: Use the three-layer system: a moisture-wicking base layer + a fleece/down mid-layer + a waterproof/windproof hard shell outer layer.

Footwear: Waterproof, high-ankle trekking boots are essential. Pair them with thick wool socks for maximum protection and warmth.

Warmth: A sleeping bag rated for -10°C comfort, a warm hat, thick gloves, and a balaclava/buff.

Food & Drink: High-energy snacks like chocolate, energy bars, and nuts; an insulated flask of at least 1L.

Other: A bright headlamp, high-UV protection sunglasses, SPF 50+ sunscreen, trekking poles, a power bank, and some cash.
